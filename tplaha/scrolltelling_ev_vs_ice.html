Charging Ahead or Burning Out?

Visualizing the Environmental Trade-Offs Between EVs and ICEs

EVs and ICE vehicles share the same roads today but lead to very different environmental outcomes. This scrollytelling page follows both lanes to ask: when are EVs clearly better, and where do their hidden trade-offs show up?

EV Lane · Pro-EV Perspective

Where EVs Shine

When the electricity mix is decarbonizing, EVs can significantly reduce climate and health impacts compared with conventional ICE vehicles. Their efficiency and fuel flexibility make them central to most net-zero transport scenarios.

Key Environmental Advantages

  • Zero tailpipe exhaust: No on-road NOₓ, PM, or CO₂ emissions, improving urban air quality.
  • High drivetrain efficiency: Electric motors can be 3–4× more efficient than ICE engines.
  • Gets cleaner over time: As the grid adds more solar and wind, each kilometre driven emits less.
  • Local co-benefits: Lower noise, heat, and smell in dense city streets.

Re-Centering the Road

The EV lane tells a compelling story: large emission reductions are possible if we clean up the grid and deploy EVs widely. But the picture is incomplete. Before we move into the ICE lane, we pause here in the center to ask: what are the system-level costs of scaling EVs?

ICE Lane · EV Trade-Offs & Constraints

Where the EV Story Gets Harder

EVs are not impact-free. Their benefits depend heavily on how electricity is produced, how batteries are mined and manufactured, and how charging is managed at scale. On this lane, we focus on the pressures and risks that accompany rapid EV adoption.

Environmental & System-Level Concerns

  • Battery production footprint: Mining and refining lithium, nickel, and cobalt can be energy-intensive and socially disruptive if not strictly regulated.
  • Grid demand and timing: Unmanaged EV charging can increase evening peaks and require additional fossil generation capacity.
  • Coal-heavy grids: In some regions, lifecycle CO₂ for EVs can approach that of efficient hybrids or ICEs.
  • End-of-life management: Reuse, second-life applications, and recycling systems are still scaling and uneven across countries.

Final Chapter: Charging Ahead, Carefully

The EV lane and ICE lane are not two separate realities; they are different ways of looking at the same transition. EVs can make road transport dramatically cleaner, especially when paired with aggressive grid decarbonization and responsible battery supply chains.

At the same time, the ICE lane reminds us that technology alone cannot solve structural problems like fossil-heavy electricity, weak regulation, or inequitable access. EVs are a powerful tool, but not a magic solution.

By scrolling through both lanes, you have seen how the answer to “Are EVs better?” depends on the underlying systems they plug into. The real design question becomes: What kind of energy and mobility system are we building around these vehicles?
